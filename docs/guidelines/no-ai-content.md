## üéØ Core Principle

AI-generated content is detectable through patterns, not quality. Even well-written AI content has telltale signs. Our goal isn't to deceive but to create content that feels natural, engaging, and human while leveraging AI's capabilities.

**IMPORTANT:** Different content types require different approaches:
- **Blog Posts**: New Yorker style - sophisticated, polished, intellectually engaging
- **Newsletters/Emails**: Conversational, like texting a smart friend
- **Social Media**: Casual, brief, trend-aware

## ‚ùå BANNED: Never Use These

### Transition Words (Dead Giveaways)

- **Moreover**, **Furthermore**, **Indeed**, **Thus**, **Therefore**
- **Consequently**, **Additionally**, **Subsequently**, **Hence**
- **Nevertheless**, **Nonetheless**, **Accordingly**

### Academic/Corporate Language

- "Delve into" ‚Üí Just say "explore" or "look at"
- "Underscore" ‚Üí Say "show" or "prove"
- "In the realm of" ‚Üí Just name the topic directly
- "Transformative" ‚Üí Use "game-changing" or skip entirely
- "Ever-evolving" ‚Üí Say "changing" or "shifting"
- "Cutting-edge" ‚Üí Say "new" or "latest"
- "Leverage" (as a verb) ‚Üí Use "use" or "apply"
- "Utilize" ‚Üí Always use "use"

### Vague Attributions

- "Many studies show..." ‚Üí Name the specific study or skip
- "Experts agree..." ‚Üí Name the expert or rephrase
- "Research indicates..." ‚Üí Cite it or don't mention it
- "It is widely known..." ‚Üí Just state the fact
- "Scientists believe..." ‚Üí Which scientists? Where?

### Formulaic Openings

- "Have you ever wondered..."
- "What if I told you..."
- "Let me ask you this..."
- "Imagine a world where..."
- "In today's fast-paced world..."
- "As we navigate..."

### Buzzword Combinations

- "Unlock the potential"
- "Revolutionary breakthrough"
- "Paradigm shift"
- "Game-changing innovation"
- "Seamlessly integrate"
- "Holistic approach"

### Em-Dashes

- **NEVER use em-dashes (‚Äî)**
- Use commas, periods, or parentheses instead
- Breaking this rule is an instant AI tell

## ‚úÖ Universal Anti-AI Rules (All Content Types)

### 1. Sentence Variation (Critical)
```
Bad (AI): All sentences are 15-20 words. They maintain similar structure. They feel rhythmically identical.

Good (Human): Short sentence. Then a really long one that goes on and on with multiple clauses, parenthetical thoughts (like this one), and keeps developing the idea further before finally ending. Three words here. Medium-length sentence to transition.
```

### 2. Number Formatting (Mix It Up)
- Sometimes: $10,000
- Sometimes: $10k  
- Sometimes: 10 grand (emails/social only)
- Sometimes: ten thousand dollars (formal blogs)
- **Never consistent** throughout the same piece

### 3. Paragraph Length Variation
- One sentence paragraph occasionally.
- Then longer paragraphs that develop ideas fully.
- Mix throughout for natural rhythm.

### 4. Brand Names & Specifics
- Not: "a popular coffee chain" ‚Üí Say: "Starbucks"
- Not: "a streaming service" ‚Üí Say: "Netflix"
- Always use specific names, dates, places

## üìö BLOG POSTS: New Yorker Style

### Voice & Tone
- **Sophisticated but accessible** - Think Malcolm Gladwell, not academic journal
- **Intellectually engaging** without being pretentious
- **Narrative-driven** with compelling anecdotes
- **Polished prose** with careful word choice

### Language Rules
- **Minimal contractions** - Use sparingly for emphasis only
- **No ultra-casual phrases** - Skip "kinda", "sorta", "basically"
- **Sophisticated vocabulary** - But never obscure for its own sake
- **Complete sentences** - Fragments only for dramatic effect

### Structure
- **Strong narrative arc** - Beginning, development, conclusion
- **Smooth transitions** - But avoid formulaic connectors
- **Varied paragraph length** - But generally more substantial than emails
- **Literary techniques** - Metaphors, callbacks, parallel structure

### Examples
```
Good (Blog): The phenomenon emerged gradually, first in Silicon Valley's glass towers, then spreading to startup hubs across the nation. By 2024, what had begun as a quirk of venture capitalism had transformed into something far more troubling.

Bad (Blog): So basically, this thing started in Silicon Valley, right? And then it kinda spread everywhere. Crazy how it happened.
```

## üìß NEWSLETTERS/EMAILS: Conversational Style

### Voice & Tone
- **Like texting a smart friend** who gets finance
- **Personal and relatable** without being overly familiar
- **Energy and personality** throughout
- **Direct and punchy** delivery

### Language Rules
- **Contractions everywhere** - don't, won't, it's, can't
- **Casual transitions** - "Here's the thing...", "But wait..."
- **Informal phrases OK** - "kinda", "basically" (sparingly)
- **Sentence fragments welcome** - For emphasis. Like this.

### Structure
- **Hook immediately** - First line must grab attention
- **Short paragraphs** - 1-3 sentences often
- **Direct address** - Use "you" frequently
- **PS sections** - Add personality and secondary CTAs

### Examples
```
Good (Email): You know that thing where you check your 401(k) and somehow it's worth less than last month even though you kept contributing? Yeah, about that...

Bad (Email): It is important to note that retirement account fluctuations are a normal part of market cycles.
```

## üì± SOCIAL MEDIA: Platform-Specific

### Twitter/X
- **Fragment sentences OK**
- **No punctuation at line breaks**
- **Numbers to start** - "3 things about..."
- **Casual abbreviations** - govt, avg, vs

### LinkedIn
- **Professional but human**
- **Personal anecdotes work**
- **Avoid corporate buzzwords**
- **Line breaks for readability**

### Instagram
- **Conversational captions**
- **Emoji placement varied** 
- **Questions to drive engagement**
- **Hashtags at end or comment**

## üîç Detection Test Checklist

Before publishing, check:

1. **Word Search**: Ctrl+F for all banned words
2. **Em-dash Check**: Search for ‚Äî and replace
3. **Sentence Length**: Are 3+ sentences in a row similar length?
4. **Paragraph Scan**: Any 3+ paragraphs the same size?
5. **Transition Audit**: Does every paragraph start with a transition?
6. **Contraction Count**: Less than 5 per page? Add more.
7. **Brand Test**: Any generic references that could be specific?
8. **Number Check**: Are all numbers formatted the same?
9. **Grammar Perfect?**: If yes, mess something up.
10. **Voice Consistent?**: If yes, add personality quirks.

## ü§ñ AI Prompt Engineering

### For Blog Posts (New Yorker Style):
```
Write sophisticated, narrative-driven content that engages intellectually without being pretentious.
- Channel Malcolm Gladwell or Michael Lewis style
- Use minimal contractions (only for emphasis)
- NO casual phrases like "kinda", "basically", "sorta"
- Build compelling narratives with specific examples
- Vary sentence length but maintain polished prose
- Never use: Moreover, Furthermore, Indeed, Thus, Therefore
- Never use em-dashes (‚Äî) - use commas or parentheses
- Include specific brands, dates, and places
```

### For Newsletters/Emails (Conversational):
```
Write like you're texting a smart friend who gets finance.
- Use contractions everywhere (don't, won't, it's)
- Start with "You know that thing where..." occasionally
- Include fragments. Like this.
- Mix formal insights with casual observations
- Use "Here's the thing..." or "But wait..." transitions
- Mix number formats ($10k vs $10,000)
- Reference specific brands (Chipotle, Netflix, etc.)
- Short paragraphs (1-3 sentences often)
```

### For Social Media:
```
Platform-appropriate casual writing.
- Fragments and incomplete sentences OK
- Start with numbers: "3 things..."
- Use trending references
- Keep it brief and punchy
- Emoji placement varied
- Match platform conventions
```

## üí° Pro Tips

### The 80/20 Rule

- 80% should follow proper grammar
- 20% should break rules naturally

### The Personality Injection

Every 500 words, add something unexpectedly human:

- A random observation
- A self-deprecating joke
- An admission of uncertainty
- A specific personal reference
- A trending meme reference

### The Specificity Test

Vague: "I went to get coffee"  
Specific: "I hit up the Starbucks on 4th Street"

Vague: "It was expensive"  
Specific: "It was $47.83 after tip"

### The Voice Wobble

Perfect AI maintains one voice. Humans shift:

- Professional paragraph
- Suddenly casual sentence. Wild, right?
- Back to professional
- Random tangent about lunch
